Introduction: Geopolitical Escalation as Systemic Market Risk

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel jointly launched Operation Epic Fury, a large-scale coordinated air campaign targeting nearly 900 sites across Iran, including nuclear enrichment facilities, missile production infrastructure, air defense networks, and military command nodes. The operation followed months of diplomatic stalemate over Iran's advanced uranium enrichment program and a succession of Iranian-backed proxy attacks on U.S. and Israeli regional interests. The death of Iranian Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in the opening strikes, confirmed in early March, elevated the conflict from a targeted military engagement to a full-spectrum geopolitical upheaval with deeply uncertain succession dynamics within the Islamic Republic.

By 9 March 2026, the operational escalation had produced its most consequential economic consequence: the functional closure of the Strait of Hormuz. This narrow 21-mile maritime chokepoint between Oman and Iran serves as the world's single most critical oil transit corridor. Approximately 20–21 million barrels per day (bpd) of crude oil, condensate, and petroleum products — representing approximately 20% of total global oil consumption — transit the Strait daily. Iran's deployment of anti-ship missiles, sea mines, and drone swarms, combined with targeted attacks on tanker navigation systems, has rendered safe passage commercially non-viable without active military escort.

The systemic risk to global financial markets transmits through four primary channels: direct inflation from energy cost increases; secondary pass-through inflation across logistics and manufacturing supply chains; monetary policy uncertainty as central banks face stagflationary impulses; and sentiment-driven risk-off repricing across asset classes. The EIA, in its 10 March 2026 Short-Term Energy Outlook, confirmed that the effective closure of the Strait has materially disrupted petroleum shipments and forced production shut-ins across Gulf states, including Iraq and Kuwait, where force majeure declarations have been issued.
